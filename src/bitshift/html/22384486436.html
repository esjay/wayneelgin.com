Do You Have Go-To-Hell Games?

I read Tom Stanley’s books and blogs about rich people and how wrong everybody is about them. One finding that should be a book unto itself is that many millionaires have a “go to hell” fund. When your job or boss stops being fun or starts being demanding, you have enough money to walk away and take your time finding something better. Everybody wants that.

PC gaming is so cheap, the cost of an Xbox 360 will get you dozens of quality games, between countless pay-what-you-want bundles and regular Steam sales. But what’s the long tail effect of so many playthings for so little cash? Lots of people have opinions on this and a surprising number of folks are worried about devaluation of games with lower incentive for high-quality work.

Of course, doing so treats digital distribution like shelf distribution which is an error of enormous magnitude. The cost of sending some bits over the internet is so low that it’s effectively zero. The more people you can get playing your game at whatever cost they can afford only cements your brand into their brain-space. And make no mistake, brain-space is the far more limited than shelf-space.

Lots of good games come from independent developers. My friends and I have this conversation weekly for the last 4 years or more.

“Man, I really want to get [new game that is coming out].”

“Yeah, it looks cool. But I already have a bunch of games I need to play.”

“Yeah, me too.”

The real effect of ridiculously cheap games delivered instantly is that it empowers the gamer with choice. Too much choice, maybe, if such a thing exists. When AAA game publishers want to charge $60 for their latest iteration and won’t drop the price for 3 years (*cough* Activision/Blizzard), my growing list of well-made, convention-challenging indie games is my quickly-depreciating “go to hell” fund. If I really want to get a new game I still can, but I’m not trapped to payÂ exorbitantly-colluded prices that consolers dole out.

That’s right: I have go-to-hell games.
