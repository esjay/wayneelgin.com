Why is Anyone Buying Apps?

Some app developers are killing it in the iPhone App Store or the Google Play Store (many aren’t, BTW). But the average cost of an app is a fraction of what even the most pointless of screensaver-collections-on-discs cost even 10 years ago. What happened?

Of course it’s a mix of occurrences such as the emergence of the self-publishing model in the post-dot-com-bubble world, the success of the iPhone leading to the personalization of technology, the fun of discovering a unique app that resonates with you, and the ease of sharing the things you’re excited about with your social graph.

While that’s not really all inclusive, I don’t know anybody who attributes the mobile app space’s success to restrictive controls and DRM over the apps themselves. Apple has controls, but Android really doesn’t. Some developers are miffed toward Google for that, but the vast majority of their customers don’t even know what that is and don’t care.

Why not? Because getting a marginally-valuable app legally is generally cheap enough and easy enough that trying to find a way around that pay wall seems too laborious to matter. They’re better served when they go along with the system than when they fight against it. Making a system that inherently rewards paying customers with ease of access and use pays exponentially better than dragging people who aren’t really your customers (even one of them) through court for settlement fees. It’s almost certainly much easier in the short term to let the lawyers go to work, though.
