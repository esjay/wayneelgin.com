Steam Sales and Indie Bundles Prevent More Piracy than DRM Ever Has

I have a bone to pick with pay-what-you-want bundles. Like a lot of PC gamers 3 years ago, I loved the prospect of snagging undiscovered games for whatever amount I selected.

Want all of Introversions titles for 50 cents? Done. 

Want Frozen Synapse for $20? Likewise.

Somewhere along the way, this good idea spawned a thousand other good ideas from our indie-bundling benefactors. You can’t walk through a gaming blog without tripping over 3 copies of Machinarium and World of Goo for every conceivable platform.

Is this a problem?

Of course it’s not a “problem.” We like choices and pay-what-you-want gives us more of them. Why then are so many in the gaming community disgruntled by naming a price? I suspect it’s the consistent, visible devaluing of the thing we paid money for not that long ago.

But, besides the customer winning, there are some more industry-wide positives to this. Namely, reducing the reasons for customers to pirate games. Steam et. al. help to reduce piracy by giving us a never-ending supply of games to play for relatively cheap. Why am I going to pirate a game that is new and costs $60 when I don’t even have time to play the hundreds of other games I already own?

Low pricing doesn’t solve all piracy (nothing will), but it has done far more to prevent the impetus for pirating a game than restrictive DRM ever has.
